## Delivery Principle

The spine is not "AI is useful." The spine is: students increasingly use AI while writing, but educational systems rarely use the resulting process data for valid assessment or teacher support. Educational Data Science becomes powerful when it connects educational theory with complex data structures and turns AI traces into evidence that is valid, interpretable, actionable, and fair.

---

## Slide 1. Title
Target time: 0:25

Thank you very much for the invitation. My talk is titled "Making Learning Processes Visible: Educational Data Science for Writing, AI Feedback, and Large-Scale Assessment." I will focus on how AI-related learning processes can become visible and useful for education.

## Slide 2. Problem
Target time: 0:45

The starting point is simple: students already use AI when they write. They prompt, copy, paste, evaluate, reject, accept, and integrate suggestions. But the resulting data is rarely used to improve the quality of education and learning. Assessment often sees only the product, and teachers rarely receive usable process evidence.

## Slide 3. Contribution to the Problem
Target time: 0:40

This is the contribution to the problem. Educational research starts with the questions: which learning processes matter, and what would count as progress? Data science then helps model nested, longitudinal, process-rich, AI-mediated traces. My work builds the bridge between those questions and evidence that can improve assessment, teaching, and learning.

## Slide 4. Structure of the Talk
Target time: 0:45

The talk has two empirical blocks that lead into a vision. First, AI feedback for writing: how assessment and feedback can make learning processes useful for teachers. Second, generative AI use while writing: how students use chatbots in PISA writing tasks. Finally, I connect this program to Dortmund and upcoming projects.

## Slide 5. AI-supported Writing as Testbed
Target time: 0:30

Writing is a strong testbed because it contains products, processes, contexts, and person-level variables. The final text matters, but so do pauses, revisions, feedback uptake, and AI-use events.

## Slide 6. Human Anchor: Science Argumentation
Target time: 0:35

Automated assessment needs a validity anchor. For the AI-feedback and science-writing block, the anchor is human expert ratings of science argumentation: 4,589 essays from 1,839 secondary students. The task and annotation manual are important here because they define what the system should learn, so prediction remains tied to theory and subject-specific criteria.

## Slide 7. Formative Assessment Foundation
Target time: 0:35

The theoretical foundation is formative assessment. In the model John Hattie and I submitted, students ask four questions while working: where am I going, how am I going, where to next, and who am I becoming in this process? These questions connect assessment, feedback, and learning development.

## Slide 8. GENIUS/DARIUS
Target time: 0:35

GENIUS/DARIUS provides the intervention context: argumentation instruction in science, with writing tasks, criteria, feedback, revision, and teacher support.

## Slide 9. Research Question
Target time: 0:35

The question is not only whether AI feedback works. The more educationally relevant question is who benefits from it, and under which student characteristics.

## Slide 10. Study Design
Target time: 0:35

Students write, receive AI feedback or self-feedback prompts, revise, and report motivation and engagement. This lets us observe feedback as a process.

## Slide 11. Main Result
Target time: 0:35

AI feedback increases engagement during revision. This is important because feedback can only improve writing when students actually use it.

## Slide 12. Mechanism
Target time: 0:40

The mechanism is feedback uptake. Feedback quality matters when students perceive it as useful and transform it into engagement and revision. The reference pills make explicit which parts of the model are already supported by publications on usefulness, student moderators, engagement, outcomes, and self-beliefs.

## Slide 13. Differential Effects
Target time: 0:40

Students with lower argumentation skills and lower motivation benefited more from pedagogical AI feedback. This is the adaptivity and fairness question.

## Slide 14. KISS-Pro
Target time: 0:30

I close the AI-feedback block with transfer to teacher professionalization. KISS-Pro asks how teachers can work with AI-based feedback systems and writing agents in language and writing instruction.

## Slide 15. Teacher Online Course
Target time: 0:30

The teacher online course shows transfer at scale: less correcting, more teaching. The point is to move from isolated AI tools toward pedagogically usable feedback practices.

## Slide 16. Interim
Target time: 0:25

The interim conclusion is that feedback is a learning process, not a treatment label. We need process indicators, heterogeneity models, and fairness checks. The BMBFSJ call is a natural next collaboration opportunity because it connects AI-supported feedback, school development, and Dortmund's strengths.

## Slide 17. AI Chats
Target time: 0:25

The second block shifts from designed AI feedback to free AI use. Students do not only receive feedback; they also use chatbot text inside writing tasks.

## Slide 18. Human Anchor: Foreign-Language Argumentation
Target time: 0:35

Again, I start the data-science part with a human anchor. For the PISA and AI-chat block, the anchor is foreign-language argumentation: 2,314 TOEFL essays from year 11 students in Germany and Switzerland, with analytic and holistic expert ratings. These criteria define what counts as quality before process traces are interpreted.

## Slide 19. PISA Data Source
Target time: 0:50

In the German PISA 2025 extension, students wrote texts with and without AI support. This gives a controlled window into AI-supported writing in a large-scale assessment setting.

## Slide 20. PISA Process Evidence
Target time: 1:15

Texts written with chatbot support receive higher scores, but product evidence is not enough. The three buttons open process analyses: writing activity, paste events, and argument coding. Together they show how AI use becomes interpretable process evidence.

## Slide 21. Argumentation Course
Target time: 0:30

Before the Dortmund vision, I return to transfer. The student course shows that the same evidence base can support scalable learning opportunities, not only assessment research.

## Slide 22. Dortmund Connection
Target time: 0:35

This is why Dortmund is such a strong fit. IFS brings the school-based educational questions, and the Faculty of Statistics brings the methodological backbone.

## Slide 23. TRACE-G
Target time: 0:35

TRACE-G is the next step for teacher-side assessment competence: how teachers can be trained to assess German and human-AI-written texts.

## Slide 24. TRACE
Target time: 0:30

TRACE shows the concrete training environment behind this vision. Teachers practice diagnostic judgments against expert-based criteria and learn which features of writing and argumentation matter.

## Slide 25. Leseband+
Target time: 0:30

Leseband+ extends the same logic into primary-school reading development: evidence for teacher assessment and instruction.

## Slide 26. Generational AI
Target time: 0:30

Generational AI broadens the program to learning and well-being with generative AI in an international research network.

## Slide 27. Contribution Network
Target time: 0:25

The agenda is already embedded in a collaboration network across educational psychology, subject didactics, computational linguistics, learning analytics, and assessment.

## Slide 28. Dortmund Agenda
Target time: 0:45

The agenda has four strands: adaptive instruction, learning analytics, large-scale assessment, and methods and measurement. Together they define Educational Data Science as a research area.

## Slide 29. Thank You
Target time: 0:40

I close by thanking the students, colleagues, and cooperation partners who make this work possible. The core message is that Educational Data Science should make AI-supported learning processes visible, valid, actionable, and fair.
